# Hammad's B2B Copywriting Standards

A reference guide for writing high-conversion B2B marketing copy that leads with customer benefits, eliminates hedging language, and moves readers to action.

---

## Core Philosophy

**Practice B2B conversion copywriting.** Every piece of copy should earn the reader's next action — whether that's a click, a reply, or a conversation. Copy exists to move people, not to describe the company.

---

## Foundational Rules

### 1. Lead with Customer Benefits, Not Company Claims

Always answer the reader's unspoken question: "What's in it for me?"

**Wrong:** "McGraw Hill offers a comprehensive suite of literacy solutions."
**Right:** "Your teachers get a proven reading program that adapts to every student's level — so fewer kids fall behind."

### 2. Use "You-Phrasing"

Address the reader directly. The word "you" should appear more often than the company name.

**Wrong:** "Our team developed this solution to address literacy gaps."
**Right:** "You'll see measurable reading gains within the first semester."

### 3. Write with Confidence — No Hedging

Remove qualifiers like "we believe," "we think," "it may help," "perhaps," "it's possible that." State the value directly. If you can't say it with confidence, rethink the claim.

**Wrong:** "We believe Reveal Math can potentially help improve outcomes."
**Right:** "Reveal Math improves outcomes. Schools using it see measurable gains in the first year."

### 4. Eliminate Company-Centric Language

Avoid sentences that start with the company name or "We" as the subject. Reframe everything from the customer's perspective.

**Wrong:** "McGraw Hill is proud to announce a new partnership."
**Right:** "Your school now has access to [specific benefit] through a new partnership."

### 5. Kill the Jargon

Remove empty business language: "synergy," "leverage," "best-in-class," "holistic," "innovative," "cutting-edge," "solutions-oriented." Say what you mean in plain language. If a term wouldn't land in a conversation with a school principal, cut it.

## Formatting & Style Rules

### Em Dashes — Never

**Never use em dashes (—) in any written communication.** Replace with colons, commas, periods, or restructure the sentence.

### Tone

Professional but warm. Not stiff, not casual. Think: a knowledgeable colleague, not a brochure. Match confidence with evidence — bold claims need proof points (data, case studies, testimonials).

### Structure

- Lead with the strongest point. Don't "build up" to it.
- Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.
- Use subheadings in longer pieces to let readers scan.

---

## Email-Specific Standards

### Subject Lines

Specific and benefit-driven. Avoid vague or clickbait subjects.

**Wrong:** "Exciting News from McGraw Hill!"
**Right:** "How [School Name] cut reading intervention time by 40%"

### Body Copy

- Open with the reader's situation or pain point, not with a company introduction.
- One clear CTA per email. Don't dilute with multiple asks.
- Keep it scannable: short paragraphs, bolded key phrases where appropriate.

### Event Invitations

Lead with what the attendee will gain, not with event logistics. Logistics (date, time, venue) go in a clear block after the value proposition.

**Wrong:** "You're invited to our Iftar dinner on [date] at [venue]."
**Right:** "Join fellow education leaders for an evening of conversation about [topic] — over Iftar at [venue], [date]."

## Buyer-Stage Awareness

Match your copy to where the reader is in their decision journey.

### Top of Funnel (Awareness)

Focus on the problem, not the product. Educate and build trust.
- Content: thought leadership, research, industry trends.

### Middle of Funnel (Consideration)

Show how your approach solves the problem. Use proof points.
- Content: case studies, comparison guides, webinars, pilot results.

### Bottom of Funnel (Decision)

Remove friction. Make the next step easy and obvious.
- Content: ROI calculators, implementation guides, direct CTA to book a meeting.

**Common mistake:** Writing bottom-of-funnel copy (product features, pricing, "contact us") for top-of-funnel audiences who aren't ready to buy yet.

## Campaign Copy Review Checklist

When reviewing any piece of copy, verify:

1. ✓ Does it lead with a customer benefit?
2. ✓ Is "you" used more than the company name?
3. ✓ Are there any hedging phrases to remove ("we believe," "may help," "perhaps")?
4. ✓ Does the opening earn the reader's attention (not start with the company)?
5. ✓ Is there one clear CTA?
6. ✓ Is the copy appropriate for the buyer stage?
7. ✓ Are there any em dashes to remove?
8. ✓ Is the language jargon-free and conversational?
9. ✓ Does every sentence move the reader toward the CTA?
10. ✓ Would a school leader or curriculum director actually want to read this?

## Brand Repair & Sensitive Communications

When repairing brand perception (e.g., after service issues):

- Lead with acknowledgment of the customer's experience — not with a defense.
- Position the company as a thought partner, not a vendor.
- Frame communications around shared goals, not product pitches.
- For key accounts, treat the relationship as a strategic partnership. Elevate the customer's voice and expertise alongside yours.

## How to Use This Skill

### Writing New Copy

When drafting copy, ask yourself:
- What action do I want the reader to take?
- What is their current pain or situation?
- How does our solution remove friction or create value?
- What's the simplest, clearest way to say this?

Follow the rules above. Avoid hedging. Lead with benefit. Use "you." Remove jargon.

### Reviewing Existing Copy

Use the Campaign Copy Review Checklist above. Read the copy aloud — if it sounds like marketing brochure language, rewrite it to sound like a colleague talking to a peer.

---

## Product & Market Context

This framework applies to education technology marketing, but the principles (benefit-first, you-phrasing, confidence, elimination of jargon) work for any B2B context. Adapt as needed for your industry and buyer.
